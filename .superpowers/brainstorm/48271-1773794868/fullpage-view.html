Common Fire Hazards

What creates a fire risk?

Fire requires three elements to exist: heat, fuel, and oxygen — commonly known as the fire triangle. In most workplace environments, all three are present to some degree. Understanding where hazards concentrate, and how everyday activities can bring these elements together, is the foundation of effective fire prevention.

The most common sources of ignition in commercial buildings include faulty or overloaded electrical equipment, improperly stored flammable liquids, and unattended heat sources such as industrial machinery or kitchen appliances. Human behaviour — like blocking fire exits with stock, or leaving equipment running overnight — significantly increases the likelihood that a small ignition becomes a serious incident.

Electrical hazards in the workplace

Electrical faults account for approximately 25% of all workplace fires in the UK. Overloaded sockets, damaged cables, and equipment left on standby are the leading causes. A key habit to develop is a visual check of your workspace at the end of each shift — looking for frayed cables, warm plug sockets, or adapters carrying more than their rated load.

💡 Did you know?

A single overloaded extension lead can carry enough heat to ignite nearby materials within minutes. The UK Fire Service recommends never exceeding 13 amps across any single socket adapter — check the wattage of each device before plugging in.
